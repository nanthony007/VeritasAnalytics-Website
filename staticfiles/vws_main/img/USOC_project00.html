Engaging Athletes Strategy¶

In order to further motivate athletes to complete the surveys I suggest utilizing John Keller's ARCS model of learning (Keller, 1987). citation below, article attached

The ARCS model is based upon four foundational prinicples of motivation: Attention, Releveance, Confidence, and Satisfaction. While developed for students, the theory is easily transferable to athletes. Coaches can easily bring attention to the issue by taking a few minutes after a workout to fill out the surveys. This could result in some data-contamination; however, if the coach takes it seriously and encourages honesty there shouldn't be a problem. These surveys directly relate to these athletes. Perhaps explicitly stating how their responses will affect furture training schedules would be another good step to reinforce the relevance of the task. Allowing athletes to build confidence in filling out surveys may seem strange. However, it is confidence in the survey and what it means that should be focused on. For example, sitting down with an athlete and explaining the factors that lead to a more positive performance experience (i.e. sleep, fatigue, stress, etc.) and checking in regularly to make sure the athlete is following the plan to control those factors will allow the athlete to build confidence by knowing they are properly preparing themselves to perform well. Satisfaction will come when an athlete follows the factor-control plan and experiences (or should experience) postive performances or percieved performance.

Finally, I would reccomend developing a mobile application so that the surveys can be filled out as soon as possible after a workout as opposed to at the end of the day (or even the next day). This would reduce data collection and entry times as well as be more user friendly. Furthermore, this would make the Load survey quite shorter as each athlete could have an account linked to their status and they would simply be filling out fields related to their current activity (travel, strength, conditioning, competition) or roughly five fields as opposed to the current 30. An alarm could also be set to alert the athletes to fill out the Wellness survey each day at a specified time.

Keller, J. M. (1987). Development and use of the ARCS model of instructional design. Journal of instructional development, 10(3), 2.

The Story of Athlete 11¶

Athlete 11 practices for roughly 2 hours 5 days a week. During this time they experiences peaks of fatigue and soreness that decrease their mood (below). Over the almost three months (90 days, 76 days of Wellness data, only 61 days of colleceted Load data) of data collection this athlete competed 5 times, used strength programs 24 times, practiced 61 times, and participated in conditioning workouts only 3 times. This athlete experiences high RPE for competitions and conditioning, but average for strength training and practices... posibly explaining why the former occurs less frequently. Nonetheless, perceived performance on all four exercise types (practice, strength training, conditioning, competition) is high.
